Oort Cloud:

The Oort Cloud is a huge, distant region that surrounds the Solar System. It is believed to contain billions, and possibly trillions, of icy objects that orbit the Sun far beyond the planets. The Oort Cloud is so far away that no spacecraft has reached it, and scientists have not observed it directly. Instead, its existence is supported by evidence from the paths of long-period comets.

The Oort Cloud formed about 4.6 billion years ago during the early stages of the Solar System. It is made mostly of frozen water, methane, ammonia, carbon dioxide, dust, and rock. Scientists think these icy objects were pushed to the outer edges of the Solar System by the gravity of the giant planets. Occasionally, the gravity of a passing star or the Milky Way can send one of these objects towards the Sun, where it becomes a comet.

Some examples of comets that are believed to have come from the Oort Cloud include Comet Hale-Bopp, Comet Hyakutake, and Comet C/2023 A3 (Tsuchinshan–ATLAS). These are long-period comets, meaning they take hundreds or even thousands of years to orbit the Sun. Studying these comets helps scientists learn more about the Oort Cloud and the early history of the Solar System.
